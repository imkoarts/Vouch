# Long-Form Examples

## 1. Repeated paragraph arc

Before:

> The interesting part is that the provider stores the traces. The real issue is not storage itself, but who controls the resulting knowledge.
>
> The deeper question is that the provider also scores the evals. What matters is not the score, but the judgment behind it.
>
> The overlooked risk is that the provider controls export. The real problem is not access, but dependence.

Problems:

- each paragraph announces importance
- each paragraph uses X-versus-Y contrast
- each paragraph ends with a thesis
- the document repeats one rhetorical template

Better:

> The provider stores the traces and scores the evals. Its export includes scores but not the underlying test cases.
>
> That omission matters during migration because the customer can compare old scores, but cannot rerun the same tests on another model.

Each paragraph now has a distinct job: facts, then mechanism.

## 2. Recap transition

Before:

> The export omits the test cases.
>
> Because the test cases are omitted, the customer cannot reproduce the evaluation. This brings us to portability.

After:

> The export omits the test cases, so the customer cannot rerun the evaluation on another model.

## 3. Empty bridge

Before:

> This raises another important question.
>
> Who can reuse the correction history?

After:

> The contract does not say who can reuse the correction history.

Use a question only when information is genuinely being requested.

## 4. Example followed by universal lesson

Before:

> One team lost its eval history during migration. This proves that every company needs to own the entire learning stack.

After:

> One team lost its eval history because the export contained scores but not test cases.

The example supports a mechanism, not a universal rule.

## 5. Abstraction creep

Before:

> The company lost several traces during migration. Those traces represent institutional memory. Institutional memory becomes organizational intelligence. Organizational intelligence is the foundation of the new operating model.

After:

> The migration dropped several traces that the team used to reproduce past failures.

## 6. Redundant conclusion

Before:

> The contract covers storage, training, and export.
>
> In conclusion, companies need contracts that cover storage, training, and export.

After:

> The contract covers storage and training, but leaves export undefined.

The conclusion adds nothing and disappears.

## 7. Valid conclusion with action

Keep:

> The export test failed because the archive omitted the test cases. Before renewal, procurement should require a sample export and verify that another model can run it.

The conclusion adds a decision and action.

## 8. Valid signposting

Keep in a complex report:

> The analysis has three parts: data collection, attribution, and contract scope.

The signposting helps navigation and carries structure.

## 9. Valid repeated terminology

Keep:

> The contract defines “Customer Data” narrowly. The same definition appears in the export clause.

Stable legal terminology is more important than synonym variety.

## 10. Valid multi-paragraph explanation

> The tournament runs for two weeks and waives combo fees during the event. That should produce more activity than the product sees under normal conditions.
>
> The useful measure is not raw volume alone. Polymarket can compare how often traders open the market, inspect the legs, and leave without placing a trade.
>
> If abandonment rises as combinations get longer, the interface may need to show the contribution of each leg more clearly.

The paragraphs perform different jobs: context, measurement, implication. They do not repeat one rhetorical shape.

## 11. Do not overcompress

Before:

> More legs. More confusion. Less trading.

After, when the mechanism matters:

> As more legs are added, traders need more time to understand what changes the combo price. That may reduce completed trades.

Fragments are not automatically more human.

## 12. Empathy control

Keep:

> I’m sorry you’re dealing with this. You do not need to answer today.

In a sensitive message, reassurance is part of the job.
