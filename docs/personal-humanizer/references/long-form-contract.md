# Long-Form and Multi-Paragraph Contract

Use for `X_LONG_POST`, `LONG_FORM`, articles, reports, scripts, essays, professional posts, and any text with several paragraphs.

## Goal

Produce connected prose in which every paragraph advances the document.

Long-form slop is often not caused by one bad phrase. It appears through repeated document structure:

- every paragraph announces a point
- every paragraph performs a contrast
- every paragraph ends with a lesson
- transitions restate the previous paragraph
- examples are followed by universal morals
- the conclusion repeats the introduction with larger nouns
- the prose becomes more abstract as it continues

The review must operate at sentence, paragraph, and document level.

## 1. Paragraph job map

Before drafting or revising, assign each paragraph one primary job:

- orientation
- fact or evidence
- attribution
- mechanism
- example
- caveat
- comparison
- consequence
- decision
- action
- conclusion, only when needed

A paragraph may perform a secondary job.

Two adjacent paragraphs should not perform the same job with the same information.

Delete or merge a paragraph when it exists mainly to:

- preview the next point
- recap the previous point
- announce importance
- restate the thesis
- provide rhetorical breathing room
- create a quotable ending
- make the document feel complete

## 2. Paragraph advancement test

For each paragraph, complete:

> After reading this paragraph, the reader now knows ______ that was not already established.

If the blank cannot be completed with a fact, mechanism, example, caveat, consequence, decision, or action, the paragraph may not advance the text.

A paragraph may be useful for pacing in literary or speech writing, but this must be intentional rather than template-driven.

## 3. Transition discipline

Prefer content-led transitions.

Bad:

> This brings us to the next issue.

Better:

> The export uses a provider-specific schema.

Bad:

> At the same time, another question emerges.

Better:

> The contract does not define who can reuse the eval cases.

Use explicit signposting when the document is genuinely complex, especially in reports, legal writing, teaching, or instructions. Do not ban signposting universally.

Flag signposting when it carries no information and merely simulates cohesion.

## 4. No recap-to-advance loop

Do not begin each paragraph by summarizing the previous paragraph before moving one step forward.

Common loop:

1. paragraph states a point
2. next paragraph paraphrases it
3. transition announces a deeper layer
4. final sentence states another thesis

Replace the recap with the new fact or mechanism.

Repeat technical terms when consistency requires them. Do not replace necessary terminology with synonyms merely to avoid repetition.

## 5. No repeated paragraph arc

Do not make every paragraph follow:

1. broad setup
2. manufactured contrast
3. abstract takeaway

Also avoid repeating:

- example → “what this means” → lesson
- claim → rhetorical question → verdict
- past → present reversal → warning
- three facts → aphoristic closing fragment

One such structure may be natural. Repetition makes the document feel generated.

## 6. Paragraph endings

Do not force every paragraph to end with:

- a slogan
- a verdict
- a warning
- a reassurance
- a one-line fragment
- a universal lesson
- an “and that is why” conclusion

End the paragraph when its information is complete.

A plain factual final sentence is valid.

## 7. Abstraction control across the document

Watch for abstraction creep:

> concrete event
> → pattern
> → dynamic
> → system
> → paradigm
> → new operating model

A longer text may move to higher abstraction, but every step must be needed and supported.

Return to concrete evidence after abstract explanation.

Do not replace named actors and objects with increasingly grand nouns.

## 8. Examples and lessons

An example may illustrate a point without becoming a universal principle.

Bad:

> One team lost its eval history during migration. This shows that every company must own the full learning stack.

Better:

> One team lost its eval history during migration because the export omitted test cases.

State the evidenced mechanism. Do not automatically append the lesson.

## 9. Conclusions

A conclusion is optional.

Use one when the genre needs:

- a decision
- a recommendation
- an action
- a concise synthesis of several distinct findings
- a clear answer to the opening question

Do not use a conclusion merely to restate:

- the introduction
- every section
- the central claim in more abstract language
- a slogan about the future
- who will win
- why the issue matters

Run the conclusion deletion test:

> If the conclusion is removed, what necessary decision, synthesis, or action is lost?

If nothing is lost, delete it.

## 10. Rhythm across paragraphs

Natural variation may include:

- short and long paragraphs
- plain and technical sentences
- occasional fragments
- lists
- quotations
- examples

Do not mechanically alternate paragraph lengths.

Flag only when the whole document shows a repeated visual and syntactic template, such as six equal one-sentence paragraphs ending in punchlines.

## 11. Preserve long-form strengths

Do not over-humanize long text into choppy social copy.

Keep:

- necessary context
- qualifications
- evidence
- attribution
- technical definitions
- stable terminology
- explicit structure in complex documents
- passive voice when the actor is unknown or unimportant
- summaries when the user requested an executive summary
- empathetic language when care is part of the task

The goal is not maximum brevity. The goal is that every part earns its place.

## 12. Document-level final check

Before returning a multi-paragraph text, ask:

1. What unique job does each paragraph perform?
2. Which paragraph can be deleted without losing content?
3. Does any paragraph merely announce or recap?
4. Are transitions carrying information?
5. Do several paragraphs use the same rhetorical arc?
6. Does each paragraph end with a thesis-shaped sentence?
7. Does the document become more abstract without new evidence?
8. Does an example turn into a universal lesson?
9. Does the conclusion add a decision, synthesis, or action?
10. Is the prose clear without sounding like stitched-together social posts?
